Start Here · The Basics

Think of it like this...

Big financial concepts are scary. But they're exactly like things you already understand. Here's Kerala's debt situation in plain everyday language.

🏠
Real Life Concept
What is KIIFB?

Imagine your family needs to build a new house. Your income alone isn't enough. So you take a home loan from the bank — you borrow now, build now, and repay later from your salary. KIIFB is exactly that — Kerala's "home loan" for building roads, schools & hospitals.

💳
The Loan Source
Where did KIIFB get money?

Not just one bank — many sources! Bank loans (₹7,450 Cr), Masala Bonds from London (₹5,650 Cr), NABARD (₹4,808 Cr), HUDCO (₹3,780 Cr) and more. Also two dedicated taxes — motor vehicle tax and petrol cess — go directly to KIIFB every year (~₹3,500 Cr).

📅
The Repayment Plan
How does Kerala repay?

Just like your EMI comes from your monthly salary, Kerala repays KIIFB from 2 guaranteed income sources that come in every single year: 50% of all Motor Vehicle Tax collected in Kerala + 100% of the Petrol Cess. That's roughly ₹3,500 crore per year — automatically.

⚠️
The Problem
What went wrong?

In 2022, the Central Government said: "KIIFB's loans count as Kerala's debt." It's like your bank suddenly counting your cousin's loan as your loan! This cut Kerala's borrowing limit. Big banks like SBI and NABARD stopped lending to KIIFB. Now Kerala is fighting this in the Supreme Court.

🌊
Why Borrow at All?
The Floods & COVID reason

From 2017-2021: Cyclone Ookhi, 2 massive floods, then COVID. Kerala's economy shrank. But the state still had to keep investing to recover faster — just like how a business takes a loan during a bad year to survive and grow. Cutting spending would have made things worse.

🇱🇰
The Big Fear
"Will Kerala become Sri Lanka?"

Sri Lanka's crisis was a foreign currency crisis — they couldn't pay for oil/food imports in dollars. Kerala's debt is entirely in Indian Rupees, owed to Indian banks. Completely different! Comparing them is like saying your home loan will cause a food shortage — it makes no sense.

Step by Step · The System

How KIIFB Actually Works

Follow the money — from borrowing to building to repaying. It's a 6-step cycle.

1
DECISION

Government decides what to build

The Kerala Budget announces priority projects — new roads, hospital upgrades, school labs. These are handed over to KIIFB to fund. So far 1,103 projects worth ₹86,170 crore have been approved.

2
BORROWING

KIIFB raises money from multiple sources

KIIFB borrows from banks, sells bonds (even internationally — the famous "Masala Bonds" listed in London), borrows from NABARD, REC, HUDCO. The Government of Kerala provides a guarantee — like a co-signer on a loan.

3
BUILDING

Money flows to departments → Construction begins

KIIFB releases funds to Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) — basically small companies set up for each project. Roads get built (₹39,749 Cr earmarked), schools get labs, hospitals get equipment. ₹36,610 Cr spent so far.

4
INCOME

Kerala collects Motor Vehicle Tax + Petrol Cess every year

Every time someone buys a car, pays road tax, or buys petrol in Kerala — a portion goes directly to KIIFB. In 2023-24: ₹2,469 Cr from MV tax alone. This is the automatic repayment engine — it works without the government having to do anything extra.

5
REPAYMENT

KIIFB pays back lenders from this revenue

The ₹3,500 Cr annual inflow from these taxes is used to pay interest and repay principal. The Masala Bonds (₹2,150 Cr borrowed in 2019) were fully repaid by March 2024. The system works — when the Centre doesn't interfere.

6
THE PROBLEM

Centre counted KIIFB loans as Kerala's state debt (2022)

This reduced Kerala's borrowing limit by ₹14,358 Cr for 2025-26 alone. Banks backed away. KIIFB's income (₹5,629 Cr) fell short of expenses (₹6,600 Cr), creating a gap that the state budget must fill. Kerala challenged this in the Supreme Court — case ongoing.

Myth vs Reality · Set the Record Straight

What people get wrong about Kerala's debt

❌ MYTH

"The RBI warned that Kerala will collapse like Sri Lanka"

✅ FACT

It was a research article by independent researchers published in an RBI bulletin. The RBI bulletin itself says the views are the authors' own — not RBI's official position. The RBI never issued any warning.

❌ MYTH

"Kerala's debt keeps growing and is unsustainable"

✅ FACT

The debt-to-GSDP ratio has been falling every year: 41.35% (2020-21) → 39.72% → 39.08% → 37.84% (2023-24). The CAG's own fine-print confirms: "debt has slimmed." The Domar Criteria (global standard) puts Kerala in the safe zone.

❌ MYTH

"KIIFB debt will bankrupt Kerala — it's a contingent liability of 3.9% of GSDP"

✅ FACT

Only if every single public sector company defaults simultaneously — a scenario almost impossible. Punjab's contingent liability is 5.3% and Rajasthan's is 8.6%. Nobody says they're collapsing. Kerala is at 3.9% — the lowest of the "stressed" states.

❌ MYTH

"Kerala's total debt is ₹3.33 lakh crore — enormous!"

✅ FACT

₹3.33 lakh crore is the total liability, which includes PF, small savings, and treasury deposits — money the govt holds as a trustee, not money it owes. The actual public debt is ₹2.26 lakh crore — under 30% of GSDP. The CAG classifies them separately.

❌ MYTH

"Kerala is reckless with freebies — that's why it has debt"

✅ FACT

The RBI article itself shows Kerala's subsidies were only 3-4% of revenue expenditure — never unsustainable. 65% of Kerala's revenue is its own tax income (vs Bihar at 25%). Kerala's problem isn't overspending — it's getting less than its fair share from central taxes (down from 3.875% to 1.925%).

The Plan B · How Kerala Will Repay

4 Ways Kerala Plans to Repay

It's not all doom and gloom. Here's the actual repayment strategy on the table.

01
🚗

Motor Vehicle Tax + Petrol Cess

The original and automatic plan. As Kerala grows, more cars, more fuel, more tax. This alone was growing at ~14% per year. The KIIFB CEO said: "MV cess alone would have been enough" if the Centre hadn't interfered. Annual inflow: ~₹3,500 Cr and growing.

02
🛣️

User Fee (Toll) on KIIFB Roads

CM Pinarayi announced "Plan B" — charge a user fee on roads built by KIIFB. This makes KIIFB self-sustaining, no longer dependent on the budget. It also satisfies the Centre's Supreme Court argument that KIIFB isn't "income-generating." Rollout underway.

03
🏙️

Local Body Borrowings

A smart workaround: municipal corporations raise their own loans for infrastructure, with KIIFB executing the projects. Repayment comes from local body funds — not the state budget. So the Centre can't count it as state debt. Kerala's Kozhikode was India's first municipality to borrow — in the 1960s!

04
⚖️

Supreme Court Battle for Fair Share

Kerala is fighting in court against the Centre counting KIIFB loans as state debt. If Kerala wins, it gets its full borrowing capacity back. Also demanding its rightful share of central taxes — Kerala's share fell from 3.875% to 1.925% over 25 years. Fixing this would dramatically ease fiscal pressure.

History · The Full Story

KIIFB's Journey — Year by Year

1999
KIIFB is Born

Kerala Infrastructure Investment Fund Act passed. KIIFB created to fund infrastructure outside the normal budget — a visionary move to build without crowding out welfare spending.

2016 – 2019
LDF supercharges KIIFB — ₹50,000 Cr in 5 years planned

LDF government activates KIIFB at scale. Roads, schools, hospitals funded at unprecedented pace. KIIFB becomes the engine of Kerala's infrastructure boom. 588 schools built via KIIFB.

May 2019
Masala Bonds — Kerala goes global

KIIFB raises ₹2,150 Cr through bonds listed on the London Stock Exchange. First Indian sub-sovereign entity to do this. International confidence in Kerala's model.

2017 – 2021
Ookhi, 2 Floods, COVID — Economy shrinks

Back-to-back shocks devastate Kerala's economy. GSDP growth turns negative. Debt-to-GSDP ratio rises — not because of reckless spending, but because the economy shrank while the state kept spending to recover faster.

March 2022
Centre counts KIIFB loans as Kerala's state debt

The Central Government retrospectively includes KIIFB borrowings under Kerala's borrowing limit under FRBM Act. Kerala's borrowing capacity is slashed. The CM calls it "against legitimate expectations." Banks begin to back away from KIIFB.

2024
RBI circular + banks ditch KIIFB

RBI tells public sector banks not to fund projects where the government repays. SBI, Canara Bank, NABARD stop lending. KIIFB's expenses (₹6,600 Cr) exceed income (₹5,629 Cr). Government has to cover the gap.

March 2024
Masala Bonds fully repaid ✓

The ₹2,150 Cr Masala Bonds borrowed in 2019 are fully repaid — proving the model works. Total interest paid: ₹1,045 Cr over 5 years at 9.72%. Proof of concept: KIIFB can and does repay.

2024 – Present
Supreme Court fight + Plan B (User Fee)

Kerala challenges the Centre's move in the Supreme Court — referred to a Constitution Bench, meaning the Court found merit. CM unveils Plan B: user fees on KIIFB roads for self-sustainability. Debt ratio falling: 41.35% → 37.84% and counting.

So what's the real verdict?

✓ Debt ratio is falling ✓ Masala Bonds fully repaid ✓ Domar Criteria — safe zone ⚡ Annual repayment pressure ⚡ Centre cut borrowing limits ✗ Banks backing away ✗ Central tax share cut to 1.925%

Kerala's debt situation is serious but manageable — and importantly, improving. The debt-to-GSDP ratio has fallen four years in a row. The biggest threats aren't reckless spending — they're the Centre systematically shrinking Kerala's borrowing space and tax share. Punjab has 54% debt. Rajasthan 40%. Neither is called "Sri Lanka." Kerala at 37.84% and falling — with 65% self-generated revenue and a Supreme Court case that may restore its fiscal freedom — is in a far better position than the headlines suggest.
